Exoplanets

Exoplanets are planets that orbit other stars that are not our Sun. When you look up at the sky during the night, many of those little twinkling stars likely host a planetary system of their own. These exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, can vary in size and properties (such as atmospheric components). One question many scientists want to answer right now is: "could one of these planets harbor life? Could we find an Earth-like planet outside of our solar system?".

Answering that question is possible, but difficult. Exoplanets are hard to detect. They are much smaller than their host star, and often are quite dim too. There are several methods for detecting exoplanets. The main methods are transit, radial velocity and direct imaging.

Transit Method

When a planet passes in front of a star, we can see a dip in the star’s light intensity from Earth. This event is called a transit. If that dip is periodic, then it is most likely caused by a planet. If the planet’s radius is big, then the dip is also big, since it is covering more light from the star. If the radius is small, then the dip is small. Big intervals between one transit and another indicate that the planet is far from its star, short intervals indicate that the planet is close to its star.

Radial Velocity Method

In the 1990s, scientists started an attempt to find a planet around a Sun-like star. Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz, astrophysicists at the University of Geneve, built a device that could detect how much a planet affected the star’s gravity, called a spectrograph. A spectrograph is a device that can split light into its component wavelengths. Spectrographs can be used to find exoplanets. We often say that a planet is orbiting around a star, but that is not exactly what is happening. The planet is attracted to the star’s gravity, but the opposite also happens. This means that both the planet and the star are orbiting around their common center of mass. A planet can make the star wobble due to its gravitational force.

When we look at a star from Earth, we can detect that wobble by looking at a star’s spectrum. The spectrum of the star is composed by spectral lines: lines that tell us if a wavelength is absorbed or reflected by the star. When the star is moving away from the Earth, light waves leaving the star are “stretched”: we can see that the spectral lines are slightly shifted towards “red” wavelengths. We call that a redshift. When the star is moving towards us, the spectral lines become blueshifted. If that shift is periodic, then it is most likely caused by a planet. By using this concept, Mayor and Queloz built a very precise spectrograph that detected a planet with the mass of Jupiter (1300 times bigger than Earth).

Direct Imaging Method

In recent years, a new method has risen into popularity: the direct imaging method. By covering the light from the star using a coronagraph, astronomers look at the light that the planet reflects off of its star. A coronagraph uses masks inside telescopes to block the diffracted light from a star. The planet's reflected light is often very dim, which makes it hard to detect.

Instruments and Adaptive Optics

Currently, many of our observations for exoplanet detection are ground based (our Telescopes are on Earth and not in space). This means that the Earth's atmosphere can interfere on our images due to its turbulence. The atmosphere severely limits the power of large telescopes, because it distorts the incoming light (called a wavefront), generating something called wavefront error. This limitation led astronomers to develop a technology called adaptive optics, which uses deformable mirrors to correct the turbulences introduced by the Earth's atmosphere (or correct for the wavefront error). The adaptive optics technology is constantly being improved, and is essential for ground based instruments that are searching for exoplanets.
